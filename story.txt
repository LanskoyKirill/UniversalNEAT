Our world is a beautiful and complicated place. It is a giant ball of rock and water, spinning through the vast, dark emptiness of space. We call it Earth, and it is our home, the only home we have ever known. To understand our entire world, we have to think about its long, long history, the amazing variety of life on it, and the story of the people who live here.\
A very, very long time ago, there was no Earth. There was only dust and gas floating around a young star we now call the Sun. Over millions of years, this dust and gas began to stick together, pulled by a force we cannot see called gravity. It clumped into bigger and bigger pieces, until it formed our planet. In the beginning, it was a fiery ball of melted rock, with no air and no water. It was not a place where anything could live.\
But over even more millions of years, things began to change. The planet slowly cooled. Volcanoes shot hot gases into the sky, creating a very early, thin air. And something incredible happened. Water, in the form of steam, came from those volcanoes. When the Earth cooled enough, the steam turned into clouds, and it began to rain. It rained for thousands of years. The water filled the low places on the planet, and our great oceans were born.\
In these warm, young oceans, something even more amazing began. Simple, tiny things started to form. They were the first life. We are not exactly sure how it happened, but small parts inside the water came together to make the first living cells. These were so small you would need a strong glass to see them. For a very, very long time, this was all the life there was on Earth \'96 just tiny, simple life in the water.\
Then, over billions of years, life slowly became more complex. It learned a new trick: how to use the light of the sun to make food. This was a huge step. These new life forms, called plants, also began to fill the air with a gas called oxygen. This changed the air completely and made it possible for other kinds of life to develop. More complicated creatures appeared in the sea \'96 animals with shells, then animals with backbones, and finally, the first fish.\
Life was still only in the water. But around 400 million years ago, some brave plants began to grow on the land. They were small at first, but they were the first to leave the ocean. Insects followed them, eating the plants. Then, a kind of fish developed legs and lungs and began to crawl out of the water too. These were the first animals to live on land. The world became filled with giant forests, huge insects, and strange amphibians.\
This was followed by the age of reptiles. Some of these reptiles grew to an enormous size. We call them dinosaurs. For over 150 million years, they were the main animals on the Earth. There were giant ones that ate plants and huge, scary ones that ate meat. They lived all over the world. But their time came to a sudden and violent end. A giant rock from space, an asteroid, hit the Earth. It caused huge fires, earthquakes, and giant waves. It threw so much dust into the air that the sun was blocked for years. The world became cold and dark, and the dinosaurs could not survive. Most of them died.\
With the dinosaurs gone, a new kind of animal had a chance to grow and change. These were small, furry creatures called mammals. Over millions of years, they grew bigger and became many different kinds. Some stayed on the ground, some climbed trees, and some even went back into the sea, becoming animals like whales and dolphins.\
And then, in one part of the world, in Africa, a new kind of mammal began to walk on two legs. These were the first humans. They were not like us at first. They were smarter than other animals. They learned to make simple tools from stone and to use fire. Fire kept them warm, scared away wild animals, and let them cook food, which was easier to eat. These early humans were strong and clever, and they slowly began to spread out from Africa to other parts of the world.\
For a very long time, humans lived in small groups, moving from place to place to hunt animals and find plants to eat. This is called being hunter-gatherers. Then, around 10,000 or 12,000 years ago, something changed everything. People learned to farm. They figured out how to plant seeds and grow their own food, like wheat and rice. They also learned to tame wild animals, like goats and cows, to have a steady supply of meat and milk.\
Farming was a massive change. People no longer had to move around all the time. They could stay in one place. They built small villages, which grew into towns, and then into the first cities. With more food, more people could live. Not everyone had to spend all their time finding food. Some people could become builders, weavers, potters, or leaders. They invented writing to keep track of things, and they made laws to live by. The first great civilizations were born in places with good land for farming, like near the rivers in Egypt, Mesopotamia, India, and China.\
These civilizations grew and changed over thousands of years. They built great cities, huge temples, and pyramids. They fought wars with each other over land and resources. They also traded with each other, exchanging goods and ideas. Empires rose and fell. The Romans built a huge empire around the Mediterranean Sea. In the Americas, great civilizations like the Maya and the Inca built large cities and had advanced knowledge of the stars and of mathematics.\
Ideas about religion also spread across the world. In India, Hinduism and Buddhism began. In China, thinkers shared the ideas of Confucius. In the Middle East, a belief in one God grew into the religion of Judaism. Later, Christianity and Islam also began there and spread to many other parts of the world. These religions shaped how billions of people lived their lives and understood the world.\
For a long time, most people in the world lived in small villages and worked the land. They did not travel far. They had kings and queens who ruled over them. Then, about 500 years ago, a time called the Renaissance in Europe led to a new age of exploration. People built better ships and learned to sail across the great oceans. Explorers from Europe sailed to the Americas and found lands and people that were new to them. They also sailed around Africa to reach Asia.\
This started a time of great change, but also of great trouble. European countries took control of lands in the Americas, Africa, and Asia. They took valuable things like gold, silver, and spices. They also started the terrible practice of taking people from their homes in Africa to be slaves, forcing them to work on farms in the Americas. This period, called colonialism, shaped the world we live in today, creating both wealth for some and deep pain and problems for others that are still felt.\
About 250 years ago, another huge change began in a part of England. People invented machines that could do work much faster than people could. This was the Industrial Revolution. Machines for making cloth, and later, steam engines that could power trains and boats, changed everything. People moved from the countryside to cities to work in factories. Cities grew bigger and bigger. New ways to communicate, like the telegraph, were invented. Life became faster and more connected.\
But this progress also brought new problems. Working in factories was often hard and dangerous. Cities became crowded and dirty. Countries competed for resources and power, which led to terrible wars. In the 1900s, the world fought two world wars that involved countries from all over the globe and caused the deaths of millions of people. After the second one, the world became divided into two large groups with different ideas about how to run a country, led by the United States and the Soviet Union. This was called the Cold War, a time of great tension that lasted for decades.\
In the last part of the 1900s and into the 2000s, the world has changed faster than ever before. We invented computers and then connected them all together in a huge network called the internet. This has allowed information to travel around the world in an instant. We can now talk to people on the other side of the planet as if they were in the same room. We have learned to fly through the air in airplanes, making it possible to visit almost any place on Earth in just a day or two. We have even built machines that can fly into space, and people have walked on the moon.\
Today, our world is home to almost eight billion people. We live in almost 200 different countries. We speak thousands of different languages and follow many different customs and traditions. We live in huge, busy cities and in quiet, small villages. We have different beliefs about God and about how to live a good life. Some of us have plenty of food and a comfortable life, while many others do not have enough to eat or a safe place to live. This is one of the biggest problems we still face.\
Our world is facing many other big challenges too. The way we have been using energy from coal, oil, and gas has been slowly heating up our planet. This is causing the climate to change, leading to more extreme weather, melting ice, and rising seas. Our factories, cars, and plastic waste are polluting the air, the land, and the oceans. Many kinds of animals and plants are in danger of disappearing forever because we are taking over their homes.\
But for all our problems, our world is also full of wonder and good things. It is a world of amazing natural beauty, from the highest mountains to the deepest oceans, from hot deserts to icy poles. It is a world of incredible human achievement, full of art, music, stories, and ideas that can make us laugh, cry, and think. It is a world where people show great kindness and bravery, helping each other in times of need. People from all different backgrounds work together to try to solve our common problems, to cure diseases, and to make life better for everyone.\
Our story is not over. It is still being written every single day. We are all part of this story. The world we live in today was shaped by exploding stars, moving continents, tiny life in the ocean, brave explorers, clever inventors, and the choices of billions of people before us. And now, the future of this beautiful, complicated, and fragile world depends on us. It depends on how we treat each other, and how we treat the only home we have ever known. This is our world, and its story is our story.\
Imagine for a moment that you are far above the ground, so high that you can see the whole planet at once. It hangs there in the darkness of space, a beautiful ball of blue and white and green and brown. This is our world, the only home we have ever known. It is a place of endless variety and constant change, filled with wonders both large and small.\
Our world is mostly water. If you could look at it from that great height, you would see huge areas of blue, which are the oceans. These great bodies of salt water cover more than two-thirds of the planet's surface. They are deep and full of life, from the tiniest creatures you can only see with a microscope to the largest animals that have ever lived, like the great whales. The oceans move in constant cycles, with currents that carry warm water from the hot parts of the world to the colder parts, and cold water back again. This movement helps to control the weather and the temperature everywhere. The oceans also give us rain, as water rises into the sky to form clouds, which then travel over the land.\
The land itself is broken up into large pieces we call continents. There are seven of these: Africa, Antarctica, Asia, Europe, North America, Australia, and South America. Each one is a world of its own. On these continents, you can find every kind of place imaginable. There are high mountains with snow on their tops even when it is hot at the bottom. The Himalayas in Asia are the tallest, with peaks that seem to touch the sky. There are also great rivers that start in the mountains and run for thousands of miles to the sea. Rivers like the Amazon in South America, which carries more water than any other, or the Nile in Africa, which was the life source for one of the world's first great civilizations. There are dry, hot deserts where rain almost never falls, like the Sahara in Africa, which is as big as the whole United States. And there are thick, green forests, or jungles, near the middle of the world, where it is warm and wet all year. These places, like the Congo Basin in Africa and the Amazon rainforest, are so full of different kinds of plants and animals that we still haven't found them all.\
The world is also a place of constant movement, deep underground. The ground we walk on is not one solid piece. It is broken into huge plates that float on hot, liquid rock deep inside the planet. These plates move very slowly, but when they push against each other or pull apart, they can cause great earthquakes. Sometimes, the hot liquid rock from deep inside finds a way to the surface and bursts out as a volcano, building new land and changing the shape of the world in an instant.\
And then there are the people. Of all the things on our world, we humans are perhaps the most remarkable and the most world-changing. There are almost eight billion of us now, living on every continent, in every kind of place. We live in freezing cold places near the North Pole and in the hot, dry lands of the deserts. We build our homes in many different ways. Some of us live in huge cities, with buildings of glass and steel that reach high into the air, filled with millions of people. Others live in small villages in the countryside, where life is quieter and closer to the land. We speak thousands of different languages, wear different kinds of clothes, and eat different kinds of food. We have different beliefs about God and the world, and we tell different stories to our children. We celebrate different holidays and have different customs. This mix of people, with all their different ways of living, is what we call human culture.\
For most of human history, people lived in small groups, moving from place to place to find food. Then, many thousands of years ago, people learned to grow their own food and to raise animals. This was a huge change. It meant they could stay in one place, and villages and then towns began to grow. Over time, these grew into cities and then into nations, with their own rulers and laws and armies. People learned to read and write, to build great buildings and monuments, and to think about the world in new ways. We learned to use metals, first bronze and then iron, to make better tools and weapons. We invented the wheel and the sail, which helped us to travel and to trade with people far away.\
In the last few hundred years, things have changed faster than ever before. People learned to use steam and then oil to power machines. This was the Industrial Revolution, and it changed almost everything. Factories could make goods quickly and cheaply. Trains and then cars and airplanes made it possible to travel across continents and oceans in days instead of months. We learned to send information instantly through wires and then through the air, with the telegraph, the telephone, the radio, and then the internet. Now, we can talk to someone on the other side of the world as easily as we can talk to someone in the next room. We can see events happening far away as they occur. We have learned to cure diseases that once killed millions of people, and we have learned to fly into space and even to walk on the moon.\
But all of these changes have also brought new problems. Our factories, cars, and power plants burn fuels that release gases into the air. These gases trap heat from the sun, like a blanket around the world. This is slowly making the whole planet warmer, a process we call global warming. This warming is causing the ice at the North and South Poles to melt, which is making the sea level rise. It is also changing the weather, making storms stronger and causing some places to have too much rain while others have long periods of dry weather, or drought. Our huge numbers and our modern way of life mean we are using up the world's resources very quickly. We are cutting down the great forests, we are fishing the oceans empty, and we are making other forms of life, plants and animals, disappear forever. We have also found ways to destroy each other on a massive scale, creating weapons that could end human life on Earth in a single day.\
So this is our world. It is a world of great beauty and great power, from the smallest flower to the largest storm. It is a world of amazing variety, filled with countless different forms of life and human cultures. It is a world that has been shaped over billions of years by slow, powerful forces, and in just the last few moments of that long history, it has been completely reshaped by us. We have built a global civilization, connecting every person and every place in a web of trade, information, and shared challenges.\
The story of our world is still being written. We are the ones writing it, with every choice we make. The future of this beautiful blue ball in the darkness of space, and of all the people and creatures who call it home, depends on what we do next. We have the power to destroy it, but we also have the power to understand it, to care for it, and to find a way for all of us to live together on this one small world we all share. It is our home, the only one we have, and it is up to us to decide what happens next in its long and incredible story.
